Introduction

The government's main job is to make decisions and set policies for the country.
They provide services like healthcare and education, collect taxes, manage the economy, and keep the country safe.
In Canada, we have a democracy where people elect their leaders and have a say in how the country is run.
The government's goal is to serve the people and make decisions that benefit everyone.

Lesson 1: What Is Government?

Canada's legislative branch, with its bicameral Parliament consisting of the House of Commons and the Senate, plays a crucial role in the country's governance.
Through a process of debate, revision, and approval, it works to create laws that reflect the will of the people and the interests of the nation's diverse regions and groups.

Lesson 2: The Legislative Branch

Canada's executive branch is characterized by a fusion of powers with the legislative branch, centered around the Prime Minister and Cabinet, who are responsible for governing the country in accordance with the laws passed by Parliament and with the confidence of the House of Commons.

Lesson 3: The Executive Branch

The Canadian electoral process, through its FPTP system, aims to ensure that the government reflects the choice of its citizens, although debates about electoral reform and the potential adoption of alternative voting systems occasionally emerge in political discourse.

Lesson 4: The Electoral Process

The Canadian Constitution is a complex mix of written acts and unwritten traditions that have evolved over time. It provides the legal foundation for the country's political, legal, and governmental structures and reflects Canada's values and aspirations as a nation.
Its adaptability and the inclusion of the Charter of Rights and Freedoms have played crucial roles in shaping modern Canadian society.

Lesson 5: The Constitution

Since its enactment in 1982, the Charter of Rights and Freedoms has had a profound impact on Canadian society and law.
It has been used to challenge and strike down laws that violate constitutional rights and freedoms, significantly affecting legislation, judicial decisions, and public policy in Canada.
The Charter has also played a critical role in advancing the rights of marginalized groups, shaping Canadian identity, and fostering a culture of rights awareness and constitutionalism in Canada.
